Fundamental Concepts

⚡ Projectile Motion

An arrow in flight is a projectile — it has an initial velocity split into horizontal and vertical components.

Core decomposition
v₀ₓ = v₀ · cos(θ)
v₀ᵧ = v₀ · sin(θ)

where θ = launch angle above horizontal
Why this matters: Even when shot "flat," there's always a small upward component due to sight line vs. arrow line differences.

📐 The Gravity Equation

Gravity pulls the arrow down continuously. It does not affect horizontal speed.

Position equations
x(t) = v₀ₓ · t
y(t) = v₀ᵧ · t − ½ · g · t²

g = 9.81 m/s²
Key insight: A faster arrow (higher v₀ₓ) reaches the target sooner, so gravity has less time to pull it down — less drop!

📏 Sight Picture Geometry

The angle between your sight line and the arrow is crucial. Your bow sight compensates for arrow drop.

Required sight offset
Δy = ½ · g · (d / v₀)²

d = target distance
v₀ = arrow speed (m/s)

🔵 Peep Sight Circle Math

The aiming ring creates a field of view based on the distance between your eye and the peep.

Field of view diameter
FOV = 2 · D · tan(α/2)

D = eye-to-peep distance
α = peep aperture angle
Physics of the Arrow

⚖️ Arrow Weight & Kinetic Energy

Kinetic energy formula
KE = ½ · m · v²

m = arrow mass (kg)
v = velocity (m/s)
KE in Joules

A heavier arrow carries more KE at the same speed — better penetration, more stable flight.

🌀 Spin Rate (FOC & Stability)

Front of Center balance
FOC% = ((BPL − TL/2) / TL) × 100

BPL = balance point from nock
TL = total arrow length
Ideal: 10–15%

📡 Arrow Drop at Distance

Vertical drop formula
drop = ½ · g · (d / v₀)²

For flat shot (θ = 0°)
Ignores air resistance

⏱️ Time of Flight

Time to reach distance d
t = d / v₀ₓ

For angled shot:
v₀ₓ = v₀ · cos(θ)
